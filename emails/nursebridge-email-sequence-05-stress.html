Nursing Student Retention & Readiness

Stress and anxiety do not originate
from the same place.

Understanding that distinction can affect whether a student's responses and interventions are effective or not.

A Useful Distinction

Stress

Connected to external pressures and demands

A response to something identifiable — an exam, a deadline, a clinical shift, a family obligation. It tends to ease when the external pressure does.

  

Anxiety

Connected to internal responses, fear patterns, or emotional escalation

A pattern that can persist even when the external pressure is gone — worry, dread, or physiological tension that may escalate quietly without a clear external cause.
   

From the research

In one nursing student study, 37.3% of students reported anxiety and 41.1% reported stress —

reinforcing how common both experiences can become in high-pressure educational environments like nursing education.
   

Many students struggle to recognize when external stress is escalating into internal anxiety, overwhelm, avoidance, emotional exhaustion, burnout progression, or unhealthy coping patterns. Without language for what they are experiencing, the line between the two can remain invisible until the patterns are well established.
